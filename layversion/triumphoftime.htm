The Triumph of Time...

Space and time are the two most basic measures by which we define our situation with respect to our surroundings. We go about our daily lives aware that we can move about in an east-west and a north-south direction as well to some extent up and down: in other words in three spatial directions. However, this spatial environment evolves continually, in some ways so subtly that we are not really aware of its alteration such as that caused by the slow erosion of a mountaintop. Other changes, while not always apparent on a daily basis, can only be appreciated over the course of several seasons, for instance the planting of seedlings and the subsequent crop harvest. Nevertheless, most often we are reminded of the relentless imposition of changes on our surroundings by mankind and by the interactions and changes that we personally initiate: the progress of time. So the passing of time means changes to our spatial environment. Evidently space and time have an intimate relationship with each other even if in our every day life we tend to forget that there is such a connection. Yet hard evidence of its precise nature had eluded science until Albert Einstein devised his theory of relativistic time and space. 'Relativity' showed that space and time somehow become ever more alike, as if they were two different sides of the same coin, the higher the velocities involved and identical when the speed of light is reached, although the theory could not fully explain the nature of this identity. Although this dual nature of 'space-time' is not apparent at the low velocities that we experience in everyday life, experiments with high velocity sub-atomic particles have upheld the theory of Special Relativity. A small aberration in the orbit of the planet Mercury provided an indirect confirmation that General Relativity was also correct.

Not surprisingly the relationship between space and time was found to be unlike that between two dimensions of space. For instance, in our every day world spatial relationships frequently concern distance and direction to some destination, often with a choice of different routes. If our destination were in say in a north-westerly direction, we could choose a route that goes in that general direction and even if it heads a bit too far south we would drive the last part of the trip due north until we had reached our goal. On the other hand had we initially taken an even more southerly route, then the last stretch north toward our destination would simply be longer. However, the direct distance 'as the crow flies' remains unaffected regardless which route we would have chosen: distance is invariable regardless the length of the route we take. In a sense the direct distance resembles what Relativity calls 'interval' except that the latter is calculated from time and space measurements as opposed to the former which may be calculated from the westerly and northerly route segments.

According to Relativity when we measure the time lapse and distance between two events- for instance the appearance and end of a 'falling star' that has entered the earth's atmosphere- we obtain different results on earth compared to those reported by an astronaut aboard a spaceship moving at a great speed with respect to us. It is as if the measurements taken by the astronaut concerned a different route through space-time from the one we experienced, yet each set of measurements will let us calculate the same result for the interval that characterises the falling star.

Specifically, when space and time are accurately measured in a moving environment the results will differ as if it had taken more time for a shorter distance compared to corresponding measurements obtained while stationary. In fact when the event involves velocities that approach or equal the speed of light, this kind of foreshortening effect and slowing down of time will be so pronounced that the spatial part will all but vanish while the advance of time will seem to have come to a standstill. It is the latter effect that is the source of many apparent paradoxes of which possibly the best known has space travellers visit another world, a voyage of a few years away, but upon their return find that meanwhile on earth everyone has aged decades. We will return to Relativity's mysterious interval, however, let us first visit some other worlds of our own to probe the intimate relationship between space and time. Instead of a rocket we will employ some more of our mind's imaginative powers for lift off.
