Sinusstructor

The act of observing an event involves space-time measurements in a known setting- a frame of reference. Each measurement provides direction and a measure for how far any event (i.e. a point on an Einstein interval hyperbola) is from some agreed reference direction and common point of origin. In mathematics the combination of direction and magnitude (a measure of some quantity like length) are represented by what is called a vector, in effect an arrow of a certain length and direction. Like direction signs on a traffic pole, vectors can have a point in common and provided they point in different directions in effect define the angle between the surfaces on which they lie. Hence, a vector can also be the line in which two surfaces intersect each other (i.e. like the traffic pole to which the direction signs are connected and where they in effect meet). It turns out that there is a way of connecting the vectors representing the measurements of space and time mentioned earlier, including the latter's newly identified dimensions, in a diagram. The symmetrical geometric figure that results has eight surfaces- a so-called octahedron, and is shown in the home page of this web site. What makes the octahedron so interesting is that it derives directly from the mathematical equations of Relativity (those defining the Einstein interval and its so-called Lorentz transformations). Its derivation and the fact that it can be constructed at all are the result of explanations, presented at this web site, why attempts to apply Relativity to the elementary objects that make up the material world around us failed. A simple oversight became a formidable barrier to finding the philosophical basis for measuring the space and time components of elementary particles (called quanta) in such a way that they be consistent with Relativity.

The cause for the mental block that has stymied efforts to advance Physics has no easy parallel in our macroscopic world. Nevertheless, some 'alternate reality' video games may provide some sense of the trap that science fell into. These games provide a visually simulated fantasy world with which the player interacts through the monitor and manual controls, e.g. the point-and-click of a computer mouse. However, no other physical stimulation or queues such as movement, temperature etc. are provided to enforce the player's sense of immersion into the alternate reality of the game. With the benefit of the player's knowledge of the real world on which the fantasy world is loosely modelled, the mind's imagination compensates for the missing sensations. Imagine what would happen if one were brought into the world the child of deranged psychiatrists (no insinuation to demerit the field of psychiatry is intended- it just happens to serve the present example). Suppose these delusional parents had decided by way of experiment to raise their child entirely in the fantasy world of such a video game, with as only concession to the real world the provision of nutrients, for excretion and mechanical stimulation for the child's physical development. Lacking any other clues, the child would come to accept the simulated world as its own and rationalise everything in it to be consistent with the limits of its confines. The correlation of hand movements of the mouse with motion on the screen would likely present a conceptual problem not unlike that posed by the Relativity and Quantum theories: the connection between the two would be inaccessible. Obviously, neither the action on the screen nor of the computer mouse can be associated with activities and their effect that the child does not know such as real motion and acceleration when walking or jumping.

It may be appreciated that under these circumstances it would be difficult for the child to devise a consistent model of its world and next to impossible to extrapolate the real world from the sparse queues that happen to parallel the larger world out there. Yet if it should make such an attempt it would almost inevitably use a 'bottom up' approach trying to extend that which it knows from its simulated reality to built connections to our real world. For instance, in order to come up with a more comprehensive explanation for the correlation between clicking the mouse and initiation of some action on the screen it might theorise that the sound from clicking the mouse is the medium for launching a next step in the game. If it were to try figure out the three dimensional rendering on the flat screen of the monitor it would be at an even greater loss and every movement that derives from the three-dimensions of the real world would need a two-dimensional explanation. In addition, two-dimensional objects on the screen possess 'magical' properties that lets your screen presence disappear, where in the real world you merely move through an opening or behind the object. Its theory of moving objects probably would ultimately develop such that it could explain why, while they are 'obviously' flat, movement seems to bring out a certain foreshortening of the most forward surfaces in the direction of motion (as the object moves across the screen these surfaces disappear altogether when they arrive at the middle of the screen and even though the object continues to move the surfaces remains hidden)- reaching this understanding would somewhat parallel that of Albert Einstein in his theory of Relativity.

Not surprisingly the child's attempts to connect its understanding of motion through the scenes on the screen with that of the disappearance phenomenon would be doomed. What it would need to do is take a bold step outside its two dimensional world where it will find another dimension that will let the child go through an opening itself and also let it see these surfaces without foreshortening if viewed in the direction of motion. The child would discover that shrinkage of these surfaces is but a visual effect of what we call 'perspective' and to understand both it and the disappearance phenomenon, it had needed to perceive that third dimension. All at once its various theories of its two-dimensional world would become whole and consistent, … except that … the child would also realise that what might have been an all consuming quest, its theories while sort of interesting are no longer all that important. Never mind that this new world will for instance offer the possibility of abandoning the game and starting a new one, possibly adopting another game character or playing a different game all together. Unfortunately, all the skills it learned in its two-dimensional world will have a wholly different, possibly only marginal value in a three-dimensional reality. Ironically in the process of growing up in its confinement the child had learned other things that it had never given a second thought, yet now these seem as relevant albeit as distant as its formal theories, … because before it … beyond the computer beckons a new much larger world to discover.

The above parable illustrates why science had failed to identify the conjunction between Relativity and Quantum Mechanics, oversights in each theory reinforced each other: without acknowledgement of the dimensionality of time ultimate subjectivity of reality appears inconceivable and vice versa. Once these concepts are accepted the next step follows as a matter of course: an elementary (quantum) object is its own reference frame with innate spatial and time measurements that are separate from (but not unrelated to) those of the observer and its context. Yet ultimately time measurements always relate to the observer's 'universal time' in its reference frame- i.e. a measure of an object's time is not directly accessible to the observer. Accordingly, a spatial measurement obtained from the object will originate in a separate frame from the one in which time is measured. Conversely, a spatial measurement that originates in the same reference frame as time does not concern a spatial characteristic of the object but always pertains to its position in the space of the observer. Hence, an 'interval' that is calculated from these measurements has a physical meaning that can only be related to the observer and not to the innate characteristics of the object. Nevertheless, the separate origins of spatial and time measurements will suffice to probe an object's characteristics. Aided by time's dimensionality which permits an alternate way of interpreting Relativity's equations, the Lorentz transformations will convert a measurement obtained in one frame to the corresponding one in the other frame so that an equivalent set of measurements in the object's frame may be obtained. As a result, not only will an object's characteristics be revealed, but they are also shown to share a larger common 'universal' reference frame with the observer.

For most of the 20th century this mental block sent physicists into different directions where ever more convoluted schemes were uncovered that forced the adoption of obscure mathematical language to describe them. The fact that the mathematical equations that define the octahedron apply to both the space-time geometry of Relativity and the microscopic world of quanta, resolves what had become the most vexing problem in 20th century physics: the unification of all fundamental forces observed in nature. Most importantly, the properties of the vectors that make up the octahedron and their relationships explain two 'hold-outs': electromagnetic radiation and gravity, and shows that they rest on the same fundamental principles. In fact gravity and our infinite shuttle orbits derive from the same geometrical property, while electromagnetic radiation propagates throughout space in the form of so-called sine waves (their spectrum includes light) whose magnitude varies in the same way as our space shuttle when it was bobbing back-and-forth (like a clock's pendulum). Interestingly the observer cannot perceive the octahedron in its entirety: it may observe its wave model or otherwise encounter a discontinuity that provides the basis for the quantum nature of the reality we experience.
