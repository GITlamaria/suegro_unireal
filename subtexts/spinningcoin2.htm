THE PARADIGM OF A DIME

We will demonstrate a simulation of how a point transmutes to a sphere.  Let us first consider how a circle can be turned into an ellipse then a straight-line.  However, we will depart from the usual treatment of elliptical relationships in plane geometry.  Take a (macroscopic) round object, e.g. a coin, and turn it around any diameter as axis.  Notice how the (plane) projection of the circle changes first to an ellipse finally to a line.  What has diminished the ellipse’s minor axis from being initially the diameter (equal to the major axis) to becoming zero?  We have introduced another variable on a third axis in the line-of-sight whose magnitude ranges from zero to that of the circle’s radius: the abscissa of a direction cosine whose angle is that of the coin with respect to the line-of-sight.

When rotating the object in each normal axis, macroscopic constraints compel us to do one axis in turn and the order of which axis is turned first, next and last  (and the turn’s direction) determines which surface will face us upon completing the operation.

We will now rotate the coin axially (as if it were a spinning wheel).  Note how axial rotation viewed from any angle leaves the coin's apparent shape unaffected (not so its orientation, but we will limit ourselves to topological form), while a rotation around any line in the plane of the circle will transform the coin into an ellipse of various ratios between the major and minor axes, and at an angle of inclination depending on the angles of rotation to which we had subjected the coin.  We will also note that the minor axis can be made zero so that our coin transforms into a line segment, if the rotation equals ½p (a quarter turn- 90º) or multiples of ½p (the line segment will be vertical or horizontal depending whether the rotation is in respectively the y-axis or x-axis).  All rotations thus made will commutate, meaning that successive rotations about the same axis can be carried out in reverse order with as end result the original starting orientation.

Next we will carry out two (or more) 90º rotations about different axes in succession and compare the end results of any two sequences in which the rotation of one is the reverse order of the other.  We will notice that the coin ends up with a different orientation (the effect is obscured for turns of 45º, a die will be easier for this part of the demonstration) even though we started with the coin in the same orientation! This experiment with sequential rotations demonstrates that a transformation of macroscopic objects in more than two spatial dimensions is generally non-commutative (some may find a die will be easier for this part of the demonstration).  We will also have observed that, even though axial rotations of any angle do not cause a change of the topological form, all the same they make sequential rotations non-commutative.

Now the interesting thing about elementary objects is that, being elementary they are not subject to macroscopic constraints and thus rotate in all three axes all in their own time.  If our coin could be an elementary object, instead of an aggregate of quantum objects, rotation of its radial vectors in any normal or orthogonal plane would be independent.  For instance, instead of taking turns, the rotations would be concurrent and if in phase would result in a simultaneous decrease (or increase) of each of its real component vectors.  In other words we would see a spatially smaller coin as a result!  In fact, since the coin would already be turning in each spatial dimension, we would not know its true size.  If we could repeat this thought experiment with a marble we would in fact see a shrunken sphere!  This surprising result can be simulated (although in reverse order) by spinning a coin and looking down the spin axis in the line-of-sight.  Soon the spinning coin develops a horizontal component to its spin until it lies flat before our eyes.  Initially we would see a point developing into a small disc (circumscribed by that part of the edge of the spinning disc that reflects the incident ambient light toward our line of sight) and growing in size until the coin comes to a rest.)
